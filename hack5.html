The Constitution of India

A comprehensive guide to understanding the world's longest written constitution

Adopted: 26 November 1949 | Effective: 26 January 1950

Quick Facts

Length

Longest written constitution of any sovereign country

Originally: 395 articles, 8 schedules

Drafting

Took 2 years, 11 months, 18 days

Drafting Committee chaired by Dr. B.R. Ambedkar

Sources

Drew from constitutions of over 60 countries

Key influences: UK, US, Ireland, Canada

Amendments

Amendable to meet changing needs

105 amendments as of 2023

Key Features of the Indian Constitution

Federal System with Unitary Bias

Divides powers between Centre and States but gives more power to Centre during emergencies

  • Union List (97 items)
  • State List (66 items)
  • Concurrent List (47 items)

Parliamentary System

Modeled after British system with President as nominal head and PM as real executive

  • Bicameral Parliament
  • Council of Ministers responsible to Lok Sabha
  • Collective responsibility

Fundamental Rights

Six categories of rights guaranteed to all citizens that are enforceable in courts

  • Right to Equality (Articles 14-18)
  • Right to Freedom (Articles 19-22)
  • Right Against Exploitation (Articles 23-24)

Directive Principles

Non-justiciable principles for policy making to establish social & economic democracy

  • Welfare state provisions
  • Uniform Civil Code
  • Promotion of village panchayats

Independent Judiciary

Integrated judicial system with Supreme Court at apex to protect constitution

  • Judicial review power
  • Public Interest Litigation
  • Basic structure doctrine

Secularism

State has no official religion and treats all religions equally

  • Freedom of religion
  • No religious instruction in state schools
  • State can regulate secular activities

Fundamental Rights (Part III: Articles 12-35)

Fundamental Rights are essential human rights guaranteed to all citizens and enforceable by courts through writs under Article 32.

Ensures equality before law and equal protection of laws for all persons in India.

  • Article 14: Equality before law and equal protection of laws
  • Article 15: Prohibition of discrimination on grounds of religion, race, caste, sex or place of birth
  • Article 16: Equality of opportunity in public employment
  • Article 17: Abolition of untouchability
  • Article 18: Abolition of titles except military and academic

Important Case: Indra Sawhney v. Union of India (1992) - Upheld 27% OBC reservations with 50% cap

Guarantees six fundamental freedoms to citizens with reasonable restrictions.

  • Article 19(1)(a): Freedom of speech and expression
  • Article 19(1)(b): Freedom to assemble peaceably
  • Article 19(1)(c): Freedom to form associations/unions
  • Article 19(1)(d): Freedom to move freely throughout India
  • Article 19(1)(e): Freedom to reside and settle anywhere
  • Article 19(1)(g): Freedom to practice any profession
  • Article 20: Protection in respect of conviction for offenses
  • Article 21: Protection of life and personal liberty
  • Article 21A: Right to education (added by 86th Amendment)
  • Article 22: Protection against arrest and detention

Landmark Judgment: Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India (1978) - Expanded Article 21

Prohibits human trafficking, forced labor, and child labor in hazardous conditions.

  • Article 23: Prohibits trafficking, forced labor, and begar
  • Article 24: Prohibits employment of children below 14 in hazardous jobs

Implementation: Child Labour (Prohibition & Regulation) Act, 1986 (amended in 2016)

Guarantees freedom of conscience and free profession, practice and propagation of religion.

  • Article 25: Freedom of conscience and religion
  • Article 26: Freedom to manage religious affairs
  • Article 27: No compulsion to pay taxes for religious purposes
  • Article 28: No religious instruction in state-funded schools

Notable Case: Shirur Mutt Case (1954) - Defined 'matters of religion'

Protects the interests of minorities to conserve their culture and establish educational institutions.

  • Article 29: Right to conserve distinct language, script or culture
  • Article 30: Right of minorities to establish/administer educational institutions

Significance: Important for preserving India's cultural diversity

Provides the right to move to the Supreme Court for enforcement of Fundamental Rights.

  • Called "heart and soul" of the Constitution by Dr. Ambedkar
  • Writs available: Habeas Corpus, Mandamus, Prohibition, Certiorari, Quo Warranto
  • Article 32 cannot be suspended except during emergency under Article 359

Example: Used in PIL cases for protecting rights of the disadvantaged

Directive Principles of State Policy (Part IV: Articles 36-51)

These are guidelines for the government to establish social and economic democracy, though not enforceable in court.

Social Principles

  • •

    Equal justice and free legal aid (Article 39A)

  • •

    Living wage for workers (Article 43)

  • •

    Uniform civil code (Article 44)

  • •

    Prohibition of intoxicating drinks (Article 47)

Gandhian Principles

  • •

    Village panchayats (Article 40)

  • •

    Cottage industries (Article 43)

  • •

    Prohibition of cow slaughter (Article 48)

  • •

    Promotion of handicrafts (Article 43)

International Principles

  • •

    Secure international peace (Article 51)

  • •

    Respect for international law (Article 51)

  • •

    Promotion of justice to other nations (Article 51)

  • •

    Maintain honorable relations among nations (Article 51)

Implementation Status

  • Mid-day meal scheme - For child nutrition (Article 47)
  • MGNREGA - Right to work (Article 41)
  • Right to Education Act (Article 45)
  • Various labor welfare laws (Article 43)
  • Gram Panchayats through 73rd Amendment (Article 40)

Amendment Process

The Constitution provides for its amendment to meet changing needs while ensuring stability (Article 368)

Initiation

Amendment can be initiated only in Parliament (either House)

Requires introduction as a bill

Passage

Must be passed by:

  • Special majority: 2/3rd of members present and voting + majority of total membership
  • Some amendments require ratification by 50% state legislatures

Assent

After passage, presented to President for assent (no veto power)

Comes into force on specified date

Important Amendments

1st

1951 - First Amendment

Added reasonable restrictions on FR, inserted Ninth Schedule for land reforms

7th

1956 - Reorganization of States

Reorganized states on linguistic basis, abolished A,B,C,D states classification

24th

1971 - Parliament's Power

Affirmed Parliament's power to amend any part of Constitution including FR

42nd

1976 - Mini Constitution

Added 10 new DPSP, made 'secular' and 'socialist' in preamble, emergency provisions extended

44th

1978 - Emergency Safeguards

Made emergency declaration subject to judicial review, deleted Fundamental Right to property

73rd

1992 - Panchayati Raj

Constitutional recognition to Panchayats (Article 243) with regular elections

Basic Structure Doctrine

Established by Supreme Court in Kesavananda Bharati case (1973) which held that Parliament cannot alter the 'basic structure' of the Constitution.

Identified Elements of Basic Structure:

  • Supremacy of Constitution
  • Republican & democratic form of government
  • Secular character
  • Separation of powers
  • Federal character
  • Rule of law
  • Judicial review

Judicial Safeguard:

Courts have struck down constitutional amendments violating basic structure in:

  • Minerva Mills case (1980)
  • Coelho case (2007)
  • NJAC case (2015)
